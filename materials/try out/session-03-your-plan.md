# Session 3: Creating Your Personal Time Plan
**Duration:** 20 minutes | **Audience:** University Students | **Format:** Hands-On Workshop

---

## Introduction (1 minute)

Welcome to the final session! By now, you know *why* time management matters and *what* techniques to use. But here's the thing: knowing about these techniques and actually using them are two very different things.

Today, we're going to bridge that gap. We're not just going to talk about time management—we're going to **build your actual time plan right now, in this room**. By the end of 20 minutes, you'll walk out with a personalized weekly schedule that you can use starting tomorrow.

This might feel a bit like homework, but I promise it's worth it. Let's do this.

---

## Part 1: Personal Assessment (4 minutes)

Before we build your schedule, we need to understand *you*. Everyone's time situation is different. Let's figure out yours.

### Exercise 1.1: Audit Your Current Week

**On your worksheet (or notebook), answer these questions:**

1. **What are your fixed commitments?** (Things that happen at the same time every week)
   - List all your classes with days and times
   - Work shifts
   - Club meetings or recurring commitments
   - Sports or regular activities
   
   *Example: MWF 9-10 AM Biology, TR 2-3:30 PM English, Work Sat 10 AM-6 PM*

2. **What are your energy patterns?** (When are you actually alert and focused?)
   - What time do you usually wake up?
   - When do you feel most alert? (Morning? Afternoon? Late night?)
   - When do you hit an energy slump?
   - When does your brain feel "fried"?
   
   *Example: I wake at 7:30 AM, peak energy 10 AM-12 PM, crash at 3-4 PM, second wind at 7 PM*

3. **Where's your time going now?** (Be honest!)
   - How many hours per day on social media/phone?
   - How much sleep are you actually getting?
   - How many hours per week do you spend on each class/project?
   - What's eating time that you don't realize?
   
   *Example: Instagram 2 hours/day, sleeping 6 hours/night, studying Biology 3 hours/week*

4. **What's causing stress right now?** (What tasks or commitments feel overwhelming?)
   - Any big projects due soon?
   - Classes that require the most work?
   - Time commitments that feel unsustainable?
   
   *Example: 15-page paper due in 3 weeks, Chemistry midterm in 2 weeks, work feels draining*

**Time for this: 4 minutes. Work through these now—don't overthink it.**

---

## Part 2: Building Your Weekly Schedule (12 minutes)

Now we're going to take that information and build an actual schedule. This is where theory becomes reality.

### Exercise 2.1: Block Your Fixed Commitments (3 minutes)

**You'll need:** Your weekly schedule template (provided below)

**Step 1:** Open the blank weekly schedule template at the end of this document (or create a Google Calendar).

**Step 2:** Fill in all your non-negotiable commitments in the time slots:
- All your classes (color them one color—let's say blue)
- Work shifts (color them red)
- Any recurring meetings or commitments (color them green)
- Sleep time (color them gray—yes, schedule your sleep!)
  
**Pro tip:** Block sleep from when you realistically go to bed to when you realistically wake up. If you need 8 hours and you have an 8 AM class, you need to be in bed by midnight. Build your schedule around that reality, not wishful thinking.

**What this looks like:**
```
Monday:
8:00-9:00 AM: Sleep (continuing from night before)
9:00-10:30 AM: Biology Class
10:30-11:00 AM: Free
11:00-12:30 PM: English Class
...
12:00-1:00 AM: Sleep (continuing to next day)
```

### Exercise 2.2: Add Study Time (3 minutes)

**Step 1:** Look at each class you listed in Step 1.

**Step 2:** For each class, estimate study time needed per week. A general rule: for every 1 hour in class, plan 2-3 hours of outside study. So:
- 3-hour-per-week class = 6-9 hours study
- 4-hour-per-week class = 8-12 hours study

**Step 2:** Schedule study blocks right after relevant classes when possible (you're already in "class mode").

**Step 3:** Spread study time throughout the week instead of bunching it all on weekends. Two 2-hour blocks spread across days is way better than one 4-hour marathon.

**Step 4:** Schedule study during your high-energy times (from Exercise 1.1). Don't schedule hard math at 9 PM if your brain is fried then.

**Color these blocks yellow or a different color from classes.**

**What this looks like:**
```
Monday:
9:00-10:30 AM: Biology Class (blue)
10:30 AM-12:00 PM: Biology Study (yellow) - while it's fresh!
1:00-2:30 PM: English Class (blue)
3:00-4:30 PM: English Reading & Notes (yellow) - during high energy
```

### Exercise 2.3: Add Personal Time (2 minutes)

**This is critical. Schedule it like an appointment or it won't happen.**

**Step 1:** Add meals (breakfast, lunch, dinner). Be realistic about eating times.

**Step 2:** Add one physical activity per day (even if it's just a 20-minute walk). Schedule it during medium-energy times.

**Step 3:** Add social time. Block at least one evening per week for friends, or mark Friday night as "social time"—something to look forward to.

**Step 4:** Add buffer time. Leave 30-minute gaps between commitments for transitions, unexpected stuff, or just breathing room.

**Color these purple or another color.**

**What this looks like:**
```
Monday:
12:00-1:00 PM: Lunch (purple)
4:30-5:00 PM: Buffer/transition (light gray)
5:00-5:30 PM: Walk/exercise (purple)
7:00-9:00 PM: Dinner & friend hangout (purple)
```

### Exercise 2.4: Add the 2-Minute Rule Zone (2 minutes)

**Step 1:** Identify pockets of time in your schedule: 15-30 minute gaps between classes, etc.

**Step 2:** Label these as "Admin/Quick Tasks" (tasks that take 2 minutes each, like emails, organizational stuff).

**Step 3:** Instead of letting these fill randomly, you now have designated times to handle small stuff.

**Step 4:** Any leftover time in your schedule is genuine free time—protect it fiercely.

## Part 3: The Reality Check (2 minutes)

**Now take a step back and ask yourself honestly:**

### Reality Check Questions

1. **Is this schedule realistic?**
   - Did you account for how long things actually take?
   - Did you build in breaks?
   - Is there any downtime, or is every minute scheduled?

2. **Does this match your energy patterns?**
   - Are your hardest tasks scheduled during high-energy times?
   - Did you avoid scheduling hard stuff when you're tired?
   - Is there time to decompress?

3. **Did you protect sleep?**
   - Is sleep actually blocked out?
   - Can you realistically stick to this sleep schedule?

4. **What surprised you?**
   - Did you realize you have more time than you thought?
   - Did you realize you have less time than you thought?
   - Where did all the time go?

### If Your Schedule Feels Impossible:

**Option A: Re-examine commitments**
- Are you trying to take too many classes?
- Is your work schedule sustainable?
- Do you need to drop or pause something?

**Option B: Re-examine time estimates**
- Did you overestimate study time needed?
- Can you consolidate activities?
- Is there any inefficiency you can cut?

**Option C: Be realistic about execution**
- You won't execute this perfectly, and that's okay.
- This is a guide, not a prison sentence.
- The goal is direction, not perfection.

---

## Part 4: Choosing Your Technique (1 minute)

**Now that you have your schedule, choose which technique(s) you'll use to execute it:**

**If you created a visual calendar:**
- You're basically doing time blocking already!
- Use pomodoros within your study blocks to stay focused
- Use the 2-Minute Rule for your "admin time" slots

**Decision tree:**
- Lots of distractions during study time? → Add Pomodoro timers
- Already have a full schedule? → Stick with time blocking
- Too many tiny tasks? → Lean into 2-Minute Rule

**Most common combo:** Time blocking (the structure) + Pomodoro (the focus) + 2-Minute Rule (the admin stuff)

## Your 3 Action Items: What to Do After This Session

### Action Item 1: Finalize Your Schedule This Week ⏰
**Do this by:** Tomorrow

- Take the sketch you made today and make it official
- Use Google Calendar, paper, or whatever tool you chose
- Share it with a friend or accountability partner if it helps
- **Pro tip:** Set a phone reminder for major time blocks to help you adjust

**Why it matters:** The sooner you formalize it, the sooner your brain starts working with it instead of against it.

---

### Action Item 2: Pick ONE Technique and Try It For 3 Days 🎯
**Do this by:** This week (pick days Mon-Wed or Wed-Fri)

- Pomodoro people: Set timers for 3 study sessions and track how many pomodoros you complete
- Time blocking people: Live with your schedule for 3 days and note what worked and what didn't
- 2-Minute Rule people: Commit to doing small tasks immediately for 3 days instead of adding them to a list

**Why it matters:** You need to experience the technique, not just understand it. 3 days is enough to feel the difference.

**Reflection questions to ask yourself:**
- Did this technique work for me?
- What was hard about it?
- What would make it easier?
- Do I want to keep going with this?

---

### Action Item 3: Review and Adjust After 1 Week 📝
**Do this by:** Next Friday or Saturday

- Look at your schedule. What actually happened vs. what you planned?
- Did you overestimate or underestimate time for anything?
- Did you stick to your sleep schedule?
- What got squeezed out, and what worked great?
- Make ONE adjustment for next week (not ten—just one)

**Common adjustments:**
- "I need 30 more minutes for chemistry" → Add it
- "I scheduled study at 9 PM when I'm too tired" → Move it to 2 PM
- "I have no buffer time" → Add 15-minute transitions
- "This technique isn't working" → Try a different one

**Why it matters:** The goal isn't to get it perfect the first time. The goal is to build a system that evolves with you. Weekly reviews make that happen.

---

## Final Thoughts

You now have three proven techniques, an understanding of why time management matters, and a personal schedule to get you started. That puts you ahead of 90% of students.

But here's the real secret: **having a plan only works if you actually use it**. The magic isn't in the schedule itself—it's in the discipline of following it, even when it's boring, even when you don't feel like it, even when something more fun pops up.

Some weeks you'll execute perfectly. Some weeks you'll abandon it completely. That's normal. The students who succeed aren't the ones who have perfect systems. They're the ones who keep coming back to their system, even after they fail.

So be patient with yourself. Give these techniques a real shot—at least a couple of weeks. Notice what works and what doesn't. Adjust. Keep going.

You've got this. Now go build that life where you have time for everything that matters.
